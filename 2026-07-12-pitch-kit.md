## Part 2 · The 12 hardest CXO objections — with answers

**1. "Why not just let o9 do all this? We already pay for it."**
Because a planning platform and a decision layer are different products. o9 (with PwC) gives DS Group demand forecasting, supply planning and S&OP dashboards — verified, and this blueprint treats it as the platform of record. What it doesn't give you: causal promo incrementality measured against your own matched controls, a what-if cockpit planners can safely play with because every scenario passes a reconciliation gate, forecast-value-add discipline on your own planners, and any of the six non-planning cases (documents, copilot, content, trade bot, vision). The cockpit rides o9's pipes; it doesn't duplicate them. If o9 later ships an equivalent module, the falsifier logic applies: we stop building that layer and keep the data assets.

**2. "These ROI numbers are estimates. Why should I believe ₹17.9 crore?"**
You shouldn't — yet. That's why the number is labelled, decomposed, and gated. Every input has a stated basis and a ±range; the aggregator shows conservative/base/aggressive rather than one seductive figure; and Phase 0 exists precisely to replace the five biggest assumptions (inventory norms, forecast uplift, document volumes, adoption, contact frequency) with your actuals in two weeks. The honest claim isn't "you will make ₹17.9 crore" — it's "for ₹0.9–1.4 crore you will know, with measured evidence, whether the conservative ₹9.7 crore is real."

**3. "What if adoption fails? Copilots everywhere are shelfware."**
Adoption is a designed outcome, not a hope. Three defences: (a) we seed with workflows that remove real pain (scheme lookups, policy answers, invoice copies) rather than generic chat; (b) we measure weekly *return* usage, not signups, with a pre-agreed kill threshold — under 60% cohort return at week 8, scale-up halts; (c) the two largest cases (cockpit, doc-automation) don't depend on voluntary adoption at all — they sit inside existing planner and AP workflows. Worst case, the portfolio's adoption-dependent slice is ~20% of modelled value.

**4. "What's our DPDP exposure?"**
Smaller with this design than without it. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 bites when personal data is processed without purpose limitation, consent, or security safeguards. The framework classifies data before ingestion; consumer-facing bots carry notice and grievance paths; employee personal data is excluded from training corpora; field photos get on-device bystander blurring and geotag retention limits; vendor DPAs and residency preferences apply to any external API. The bigger DPDP risk is the status quo — ungoverned spreadsheets of distributor and consumer data moving over email.

**5. "Why should an AGM own this and not IT?"**
Because every failure mode of enterprise AI is a business failure, not a server failure: a forecast nobody trusts, an approval queue nobody staffs, a copilot answering a question the org didn't want asked. IT owns platforms and security — and sits on the Council with veto on both. But use-case selection, ROI accountability, adoption, and the decision to promote a system from assist to automate are business judgments. The blueprint's operating model names a business owner *and* a technical owner for every system precisely so this isn't a turf question. Also, candidly: the three prototypes exist because a business owner who can build closes the gap between slideware and software.

**6. "We're an FMCG company, not a tech company. Who maintains all this?"**
Phase 2 institutionalises exactly that: an MLOps baseline (registry, monitors, logs), model cards, quarterly risk reviews, and an AI academy — sized in the budget. Headcount asks are modest because the stack is deliberately boring: open-source forecasting libraries, bought models behind APIs, bought OCR — the in-house IP is thin, high-value layers (rules engines, semantic layer, guardrails). And two of the eight cases are explicitly buy-first (shelf vision, WhatsApp BSP) to avoid building undifferentiated tech.

**7. "AI hallucinates. What happens when it invents a policy or a price?"**
It's designed out at the architecture level, not prompted away: the copilot must cite or refuse — no citation, no claim; SQL is generated only against a validated semantic layer with column allow-lists; the document engine's decisions come from written rules, with the LLM confined to extraction under a calibrated confidence gate; the trade bot's transactional answers are template-grounded. The prototype eval suite includes exactly these traps — version conflicts, buried details, injection attempts — and release is blocked on passing them.

**8. "Won't the unions / field force see this as job cuts?"**
The portfolio is deliberately augmentation-shaped: planners get a cockpit, not a replacement; QC inspectors get a tireless assistant with humans keeping line-stop authority; telecallers shed status calls, not relationships; the copilot gives every desk worker a research assistant. The honest line in the blueprint: time savings only monetise if managers redeploy the time — this is a capacity-creation programme, and FVA is reviewed at process level, not person level, to keep it from becoming surveillance.

**9. "What did the last consultant's AI deck cost us, and how is this different?"**
Three differences you can check tonight: (a) evidence — three of the eight cases run as prototypes with test results, not vendor screenshots; (b) falsifiers — every case states the measured result that kills it, which no sales deck does; (c) auditability — every DS Group fact carries a re-fetched citation, every assumption a basis, and the arithmetic reproduces (a red-team pass found and fixed five calculation errors before you saw this — the fix log ships with the artifact set).

**10. "Data security: our margins and pricing are the crown jewels. This puts them in an AI?"**
Only for roles that already see them. The architecture enforces scope twice — at retrieval (access-tagged corpora, exec docs segregated at storage) and at query (column-level policy: an operations user's SQL physically cannot select margin columns). Same question, two roles, two different answers — demonstrated in the prototype eval, with a zero-leak release gate and quarterly red-teams. External model APIs see prompts under DPA, not your document store.

**11. "Why these eight? My function's use case scored high and isn't a 'case'."**
Selection rule is printed on the exec page: highest weighted score, subject to ≥5 functions covered and programme coherence. Near-top scorers weren't dropped — they were absorbed: HR assistant and IT service desk are copilot skins (Case 2), secondary-sales sensing feeds the cockpit (Case 1), scheme claims and contracts are phases of document-to-decision (Case 3). The prioritiser is interactive precisely so you can re-weight and check whether the answer changes. (Under all four presets, the top-3 don't.)

**12. "What if we do nothing? 16% CAGR got us here without AI."**
Two answers. Arithmetic: organic 16% gets to ₹20,000 cr around FY30 — the stated target is 2029, and management itself names AI/automation as an enabler (V16). Competitive: this is already happening around you — HUL publicly runs shelf-image recognition at ~2.5 crore images a month, an AI supply-chain Nerve Center, and GenAI retailer marketing on Shikhar (V19, from HUL's own newsroom); at tooling parity, the edge goes to whoever converts distribution reach into decision data first. Do-nothing also has a price tag the blueprint makes visible: the conservative case values today's forecast error, document leakage and search time at ~₹10 crore a year — that's the annual cost of deferring, before compounding.
