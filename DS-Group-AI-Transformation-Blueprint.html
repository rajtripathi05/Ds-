The thesis in four sentences

1. DS Group's advantage is physical reach (35 lakh outlets, 21 plants) — AI's job is to make every node of that reach decide better and cost less to serve. 2. The fastest compounding comes from three “decision spines”: demand → supply (forecast-led S&OP), knowledge → action (a governed enterprise copilot), and document → decision (straight-through processing of invoices, claims and contracts) — I have working prototypes of all three, built on a synthetic DS-themed data spine (151 SKUs, 60 distributors, 297,610 sales rows). 3. Because DS Group has already committed to o9 for planning VERIFIED, the demand work is deliberately positioned as the causal-promo and what-if layer that complements a planning platform rather than competing with it. 4. Governance is not an afterthought: a DPDP-aligned, human-in-the-loop operating model is specified before scale, because trust — with trade partners, employees and regulators — is the licence to automate.

1 · Demand Forecasting + S&OP What-If Cockpit

Supply Chain PROTOTYPE BUILT — ds-demand-cockpit
Problem

A ₹10,000-cr portfolio V1 selling through 35 lakh outlets V9 lives or dies on weekly SKU × geography forecasts. Promo-heavy categories (Pulse alone is ₹750 cr V10) make naive statistical forecasts systematically wrong around schemes and festivals; the cost shows up as stockouts on winners, write-offs on shelf-life stock, and trade spend whose true incrementality nobody can prove. DS Group has already partnered with o9 for demand & distribution planning V13 — so the opportunity is the decision layer: causal promo ROI, constrained what-if simulation, and forecast-value-add (FVA) discipline that a platform rollout does not deliver by itself.

Data needed
  • 24 months secondary sales by SKU × distributor × week (DMS)
  • Primary sales & distributor inventory (ERP/DMS), promo calendar, price history
  • Festival/season calendar, SKU costs & margins, sales targets, new-launch analogs
Approach (demonstrated in prototype)

Hierarchical engine: AutoETS baseline + LightGBM challenger with price/promo/festival features, Croston/TSB for intermittent SKUs; rolling-origin backtests (WMAPE, bias) gated to beat naive seasonal at SKU and category level. MinT reconciliation so SKU→brand→category→zone totals tie exactly. Causal promo uplift from matched non-promo controls — incrementality, not correlation — priced into ₹ ROI per promo. A constrained optimizer trades service level vs working capital vs trade spend and names the binding constraint. Planners use a what-if cockpit (promo depth/dates, price, launch timing); every scenario is validated by the deterministic reconcile-and-optimize step before it can apply. Messy-data resilience layer: warn + fallback + completeness badge, never a silent failure.

KPIs
  • Relative WMAPE reduction vs incumbent process (target 10–20% E3)
  • Forecast bias within ±3%; FVA vs planner override tracked weekly
  • Stockout rate on flagged near-stockout SKUs; write-offs on shelf-life stock
  • % trade promos with measured incremental ROI; planner hours per cycle

2 · Enterprise Knowledge Copilot (role-scoped RAG + governed SQL)

Legal / IT · cross-functional PROTOTYPE BUILT — ds-copilot
Problem

A multi-business group (7 verticals V7, 21 plants + 24 depots V8) accumulates policies, SOPs, circulars and tribal knowledge faster than people can find them. Answers arrive by asking colleagues; data questions queue behind analysts; sensitive figures (margins, pricing) must never leak to the wrong role. Generic chatbots fail exactly here — the hard part is governance, not chat.

Data needed
  • Policy/SOP/circular corpus with owner + effective-date + access tags
  • Curated read-only data marts (product, pricing [exec-scoped], sales, schemes)
  • A written metric dictionary → YAML semantic layer; an evaluation question set per role
Approach (demonstrated in prototype)

Grounded-and-cited RAG: every answer carries doc citations; no citation → the copilot says so rather than claiming. Version conflicts resolved by effective date (newer policy wins — demonstrated on a planted 2023-vs-2025 leave-policy pair). Text-to-SQL generates only against a governed semantic layer; every query is validated (read-only, allowed tables/columns, row limits) before execution. Role scoping is enforced twice — at retrieval via access tags and at SQL via column policy — so the same question yields different, correctly-scoped answers for an operations user vs an exec. Guardrails: PII refusal, prompt-injection defence, out-of-scope refusal — all part of a 25-question eval harness that gates every release.

KPIs
  • Eval-set accuracy ≥90%; citation coverage 100% of factual claims
  • Role-leak rate = 0 (hard release gate, red-teamed quarterly)
  • Weekly active users; time-to-answer; HR/IT ticket deflection %
  • Analyst ad-hoc query queue length

3 · Document-to-Decision Automation (invoices → claims → contracts)

Finance · Sales · Legal PROTOTYPE BUILT — ds-doc-to-decision
Problem

Every distributor and vendor interaction generates documents — POs, GRNs, invoices, COAs, trade-scheme claims — that people read, match and re-key. Manual 3-way matching is slow and leaky: duplicate invoice numbers, arithmetic errors, expired COAs and out-of-tolerance quantities slip through precisely because volume is high and each document looks routine. At ₹10,000-cr scale V1 with 21 plants and 24 depots V8, single-digit-basis-point leakage is crores.

Data needed
  • PO / GRN / invoice sets from ERP + scanned/PDF variants; vendor & SKU masters
  • Written matching rules & tolerances (e.g., ±2% qty; COA validity windows)
  • Trade-scheme terms; historical human decisions to calibrate confidence
Approach (demonstrated in prototype)

Extraction (pdfplumber-first, LLM/vision fallback) with field-level confidence — but rules are truth: a deterministic match engine implementing the written tolerances decides approve / route / reject, with a human-readable reason naming the exact failing check. A calibrated confidence gate sends only high-confidence documents straight through; everything else lands in a human queue. Every override is logged and re-tunes thresholds — the prototype demonstrates straight-through rate climbing across simulated override rounds. Never a silent post: unreadable documents are flagged and routed, never dropped. Validated on 40 document sets with 13 planted discrepancies (qty breaches, price mismatches, missing GRN, expired COA, duplicate invoice, arithmetic error) — all caught.

KPIs
  • Straight-through-processing rate (target 65–80% by month 6 E6)
  • Field-extraction accuracy ≥98% on amounts; 100% of rejects carry a named rule
  • ₹ leakage caught (duplicates, over-billing, expired COAs); cycle time PO→posting
  • Human-override rate trending down; zero silent posts (audit)

If procurement prefers speed, buy extraction as an API and keep the rules engine in-house (that is exactly how the prototype is factored).

Risks

  • Vendor/distributor gaming of tolerances → periodic rule audits, duplicate-pattern analytics

Falsifier: amount-field extraction <95% after month 1 on real documents → swap extraction vendor before any scale spend.

Problem

8 · Vision QC on Packing Lines

Manufacturing STRATEGIC BET
Problem

Across 21 plants V8 making food and mouth-freshener products, packaging defects — seal integrity, fill level, misprinted/illegible date codes, label placement — are caught by sampled human inspection. Escapes become consumer complaints and, in the worst case, food-safety incidents; over-rejection becomes waste. Consistency, not headcount, is the gap.

Edge cameras + lightweight vision models per defect class on two pilot lines (one confectionery, one mouth-freshener), run in shadow mode against human QC until precision/recall is proven, then gate to reject-with-human-review, then auto-reject for high-confidence classes only. Defect Pareto feeds maintenance and material-supplier actions.

Phased roadmap — 0 · 6 · 12 months

Three phases, each with explicit entry/exit gates so spend is always contingent on evidence. Principle: shadow before assist, assist before automate — nothing takes an autonomous action until it has beaten the incumbent process in a measured trial. Prototype head-start on Cases 1–3 compresses Phase 1 materially.

AI governance framework

Governance is the licence to automate — with employees, trade partners and regulators. Five pillars, one operating body, and a risk-tiering rule that decides how much autonomy any system may have. Aligned to India's DPDP Act 2023, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, and ISO/IEC 42001 vocabulary; the prototype systems already implement the patterns marked ◆.

Operating body

AI Council — chaired by a business COO/CFO-level sponsor; members: CIO/IT, Legal/Compliance, HR, one business head per active vertical, and the AI lead. Meets monthly (weekly during Phase 0). Owns: the use-case register and risk tiers, release-gate sign-offs, the incident register, and an annual external audit. Every production system has a named business owner (accountable for outcomes) and technical owner (accountable for model behaviour) — no orphan models.

1 · Privacy & data protection (DPDP Act 2023)
  • Data classification before ingestion: public / internal / confidential / personal; personal data enters AI systems only with purpose limitation and minimisation.
  • Consent & notice obligations for consumer-facing bots; grievance-redressal path published.
  • No employee personal data in training corpora; retrieval corpora carry owner + retention tags. ◆ (copilot access-tag design)
  • Field photos (shelf vision): on-device bystander blurring, geotag retention limits.
  • Vendor DPAs for any external model API; India data-residency preference where feasible.
2 · Access control & security
  • Role-scoped retrieval AND column-level SQL policy — enforcement at two layers, never one. ◆
  • Semantic layer as the only queryable surface; every generated query validated read-only with table/column allow-lists and row limits. ◆
  • Prompt-injection and PII-exfiltration red-team suite run before every release and quarterly thereafter. ◆ (eval RF-02 pattern)
  • Exec-scoped corpora (margins, pricing) segregated at storage, not just at prompt.
  • Least-privilege service accounts; secrets in vault; audit of who-asked-what for sensitive scopes.
3 · Human-in-the-loop by risk tier
  • Tier 1 — Assist: drafts, answers, forecasts for human use. Control: citations/confidence mandatory. (Copilot Q&A, cockpit scenarios, content drafts)
  • Tier 2 — Augment: system recommends, human approves each action. Control: approval UI with reasons; sampling QA. (Content publishing, optimizer plans, claim responses)
  • Tier 3 — Automate: system acts alone above a calibrated confidence gate. Control: named failing-check reasons, override queue, auto-rollback triggers, periodic human sampling. ◆ (doc-to-decision STP)
  • Promotion between tiers only by AI Council sign-off against measured error rates — never by default.
4 · Model risk management
  • Model cards for every production model: purpose, data, eval results, known failure modes, owner.
  • Pre-release: backtests vs incumbent baseline (must beat it); bias checks where people are affected (e.g., any HR screening).
  • In production: drift monitors on inputs and outputs; challenger models; kill-switch and documented fallback to the manual process. ◆ (never-stops resilience pattern)
  • LLM-specific: grounded-or-refuse policy, hallucination rate tracked on a frozen eval set per release. ◆
  • Third-party models treated as vendors: version pinning, change-notification clauses, exit plan.
5 · Audit, logging & accountability
  • Immutable decision logs for every Tier-2/3 action: input, model version, confidence, rule fired, human involved, outcome — audit-replayable end-to-end. ◆
  • Override tracking as a first-class metric: every human correction is logged and feeds retuning. ◆ (learning loop)
  • Quarterly model-risk report to the AI Council; annual independent audit of Tier-3 systems.
  • Incident taxonomy (Sev-1: wrong automated financial/consumer action; Sev-2: access leak; Sev-3: quality drift) with response runbooks and blameless postmortems.
  • Public-facing AI disclosure standard for consumer bots — people are told when they are talking to a machine.

Scoring rubric (used in Tabs 2–3)

Business Value

5 = multi-crore recurring ₹ or board-level strategic lever · 3 = meaningful single-function gain · 1 = marginal convenience.

Feasibility

5 = proven pattern, buildable in one quarter (prototype exists) · 3 = 2–3 quarters, some integration risk · 1 = research-grade or heavy change management.

Data Readiness

5 = data exists, clean, accessible · 3 = exists but fragmented/messy · 1 = must be newly instrumented (sensors, photos, labels).

Risk posture

5 = low residual risk after standard controls · 3 = manageable with HITL + audit · 1 = severe regulatory/brand/safety exposure. Higher = safer, so all four dimensions point the same way.
